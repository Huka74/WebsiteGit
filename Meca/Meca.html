Let's take as an example a point of mass \(\textcolor{green}{m}\), restricted to move on a circle of radius \(L\), subject to gravity \(\textcolor{blue}{\vec{g}}\). Let's also add a spring of stiffness \(\textcolor{red}{k}\) attached to the mass and a point at a distance \(\textcolor{orange}{d}\) above the center of the circle.

Given the restriction of the mass to move on the circle, a natural parametrisation of its position is by its angle \(\theta\). Taking as origin the center of the circle, the position of the mass is \( \textbf{x} = L\left(\begin{array}{c} \sin\theta \\ -\cos\theta \\ \end{array}\right)\). The coordinate of the other end of the spring is \( \left(\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ L+d \\ \end{array}\right)\). The length of the spring is then \[ l(\theta) = \left|\left(\begin{array}{c} L\sin\theta \\ -L\cos\theta \\ \end{array}\right) - \left(\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ L+d \\ \end{array}\right)\right| = \sqrt{L^2\sin^2\theta + \left(L\cos\theta+L+d\right)^2} = \sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}\] With this we can write the potential energy: \[ \begin{split} V(\theta) & = E_{\textcolor{red}{k}}(\theta)+E_{\textcolor{blue}{g}}(\theta)\\ & = \frac{1}{2}\textcolor{red}{k}(l(\theta)-l_0)^2-m\textcolor{blue}{g}L\cos\theta\\ & = \frac{1}{2}\textcolor{red}{k}\left(\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}-l_0\right)^2-m\textcolor{blue}{g}L\cos\theta\\ \end{split} \] And deriving it, we find: \[ \begin{split} \frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}\theta} & = \frac{-L(L+d)\sin\theta}{\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}}\textcolor{red}{k}\left(\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}-l_0\right) +m\textcolor{blue}{g}L\sin\theta\\ & = L\sin\theta\left(m\textcolor{blue}{g}-(L+d)\textcolor{red}{k}\left(1-\frac{l_0}{\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}}\right)\right) \end{split} \] Analyzing the stationnary points, we set \(\frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}\theta}=0\). Having quite a bit of parameters, we will analyze the stationnary points step by step.

Trivial case: \(\textcolor{red}{k}=0, \textcolor{blue}{g}=0\)

Removing all forces acting on the mass leave it free to move on the circle. You can see on the canvas that the potential is constant, and so the particle moves freely (appart from friction added to the simulation).

Second case: \(\textcolor{red}{k}=0, \textcolor{blue}{g}\ne0\)

This is a simple pendulum, with potential \(V(\theta) = -mgL\cos\theta\). The stationnary positions are \(\theta = 0,\pi\), at the bottom and the top of the circle. Considering the stability of those points, we need to look at the sign of the second derivative of the potential. \(\frac{\mathrm{d}^2V}{\mathrm{d}\theta^2}(\theta) = mgl\cos\theta, \quad \frac{\mathrm{d}^2V}{\mathrm{d}\theta^2}(0) > 0, \quad \frac{\mathrm{d}^2V}{\mathrm{d}\theta^2}(\pi) < 0\) So, as expected, the bottom is stable and the top is unstable.

Third case: \(\textcolor{red}{k}\ne0, \textcolor{blue}{g}=0\)

This case is more interesting, as it shows how stability analysis can tell you how changing a parameter can make a stable stationnary point, instable, and vice-versa.


General case: \(\textcolor{red}{k}\ne0, \textcolor{blue}{g}=0\)

Two simple solutions are \(\theta = 0,\pi\), at the bottom and the top of the circle.
