Stationnary point analysis

Let's take as an example a point of mass \(\textcolor{green}{m}\), restricted to move on a circle of radius \(L\), subject to gravity \(\textcolor{blue}{\vec{g}}\). Let's also add a spring of stiffness \(\textcolor{red}{k}\) attached to the mass and a point at a distance \(\textcolor{orange}{d}\) above the center of the circle.

Given the restriction of the mass to move on the circle, a natural parametrisation of its position is by its angle \(\theta\). Taking as origin the center of the circle, the position of the mass is \( \textbf{x} = L\left(\begin{array}{c} \sin\theta \\ -\cos\theta \\ \end{array}\right)\). The coordinate of the other end of the spring is \( \left(\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ L+d \\ \end{array}\right)\). The length of the spring is then \[ l(\theta) = \left|\left(\begin{array}{c} L\sin\theta \\ -L\cos\theta \\ \end{array}\right) - \left(\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ L+d \\ \end{array}\right)\right| = \sqrt{L^2\sin^2\theta + \left(L\cos\theta+L+d\right)^2} = \sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}\] With this we can write the potential energy: \[ \begin{split} V(\theta) & = E_{\textcolor{red}{k}}(\theta)+E_{\textcolor{blue}{g}}(\theta)\\ & = \frac{1}{2}\textcolor{red}{k}(l(\theta)-l_0)^2-m\textcolor{blue}{g}L\cos\theta\\ & = \frac{1}{2}\textcolor{red}{k}\left(\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}-l_0\right)^2-m\textcolor{blue}{g}L\cos\theta\\ \end{split} \] And deriving it, we find: \[ \begin{split} \frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}\theta} & = \frac{-L(L+d)\sin\theta}{\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}}\textcolor{red}{k}\left(\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}-l_0\right) +m\textcolor{blue}{g}L\sin\theta\\ & = L\sin\theta\left(m\textcolor{blue}{g}-(L+d)\textcolor{red}{k}\left(1-\frac{l_0}{\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}}\right)\right) \end{split} \] Analyzing the stationary points, we set \(\frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}\theta}=0\). Having quite a bit of parameters, we will analyze the stationary points step by step.
\(\bullet\) Trivial case: \(\textcolor{red}{k}=0, \textcolor{blue}{g}=0\)
Removing all forces acting on the mass leave it free to move on the circle. You can see on the animation that the potential is constant, and so the particle moves freely (appart from friction added to the simulation).
\(\bullet\) Second case: \(\textcolor{red}{k}=0, \textcolor{blue}{g}\ne0\)
This is a simple pendulum, with potential \(V(\theta) = -mgL\cos\theta\).
The stationary positions are \(\theta = 0,\pi\), at the bottom and the top of the circle. Considering the stability of those points, we need to look at the sign of the second derivative of the potential.
\[\frac{\mathrm{d}^2V}{\mathrm{d}\theta^2}(\theta) = mgl\cos\theta, \quad \frac{\mathrm{d}^2V}{\mathrm{d}\theta^2}(0) > 0, \quad \frac{\mathrm{d}^2V}{\mathrm{d}\theta^2}(\pi) < 0\]
So, as expected, the bottom is stable and the top is unstable.
\(\bullet\) Third case: \(\textcolor{red}{k}\ne0, \textcolor{blue}{g}=0\)
This case is interesting as it shows how stability analysis can tell you how changing a parameter can make a stable stationary point, instable, and vice-versa. The stationary points verify \[\frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}\theta} = -L(L+d)\textcolor{red}{k}\sin\theta\left(1-\frac{l_0}{\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}}\right)=0\]
Solving this for \(\theta\), we can already find two simple solutions setting \(\sin\theta=0\): \(\theta = 0,\pi\), at the bottom and the top of the circle.
However, the stability depends on the parameters of the problem. The second derivative gives \[\frac{\mathrm{d}^2V}{\mathrm{d}\theta^2}(\theta) = -L(L+d)\textcolor{red}{k}\left(\cos\theta\left(1-\frac{l_0}{\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}}\right)-\sin^2\theta\frac{l_0L(L+d)}{\left(L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta\right)^{\frac{3}{2}}}\right)\] \[\frac{\mathrm{d}^2V}{\mathrm{d}\theta^2}(0) = L(L+d)\textcolor{red}{k}\left(\frac{l_0}{2L+d}-1\right),\quad \frac{\mathrm{d}^2V}{\mathrm{d}\theta^2}(\pi) = L(L+d)\textcolor{red}{k}\left(1-\frac{l_0}{d}\right)\] Imposing the second derivative to be positive, we find that the bottom is stable iff \(l_0>2L+d\) and the top is stable iff \(l_0 < d\).
These stability condition were expected. For example, for the top point to be stable, the rest length of the spring must be smaller than the distance between the top of the circle and the anchor point of the spring, i.e. \(l_0< d\). If it was not the case, the spring would want to expand, pushing the mass out of the top position, until its length match its rest length.
Similarly, for the bottom point to be stable, the rest length of the spring must be greater than the distance between the bottom of the circle and the anchor point of the spring, i.e. \(l_0>2L+d\). If it was not the case, the spring would want to contract, pulling the mass out of the bottom position, until its length match its rest length. This can be seen in the animation where \(L=50\). Using the default value for \(d=60\), we can see that \(\theta=0\) is stable only after \(l_0>160\), and that \(\theta=\pi\) is stable only for \(l_0<60\).

This is for the two solutions \(\theta=0,\pi\), but there exist other stationary points setting the other part to 0. \[1-\frac{l_0}{\sqrt{L^2+(L+d)^2+2L(L+d)\cos\theta}}=0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \theta_\pm=\pm\text{arccos}\left(\frac{l_0^2-L^2-(L+d)^2}{2L(L+d)}\right)\] Analyzing the stability of those two points, we get \[\frac{\mathrm{d}^2V}{\mathrm{d}\theta^2}(\theta_\pm) = -\sin^2\left(\theta_\pm\right)\frac{L(L+d)}{l_0^2}<0\] which is always negative. So those two points are always stable.
However, they do not always exist: for the \(\text{arccos}\) to be defined, we need \[-1 \le \frac{l_0^2-L^2-(L+d)^2}{2L(L+d)} \le 1 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad d\le l_0 \le 2L+d\]
\(\bullet\) General case: \(\textcolor{red}{k}\ne0, \textcolor{blue}{g}\ne0\)
The stationary points \(\theta=0,\pi\) always exist.
\(\theta=0\) is stable for \(l_0<d\left(1-\frac{mg}{(L+d)k}\right)\) and unstable otherwise, and \(\theta=\pi\) is stable for \(l_0>(2L+d)\left(1-\frac{mg}{(L+d)k}\right)\) and unstable otherwise.
For \(d\left(1-\frac{mg}{(L+d)k}\right)<l_0<(2L+d)\left(1-\frac{mg}{(L+d)k}\right)\), two more stationary exist at \[\theta_\pm=\pm\text{arccos}\left(\frac{(L+d)k}{2L}\left(\frac{l_0}{(L+d)k-mg}\right)^2-\frac{L^2+(L+d)^2}{2L(L+d)}\right)\] and are always stable.
