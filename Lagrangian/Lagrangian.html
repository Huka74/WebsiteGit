Lagrangian mechanics

Classical mechanics is often treated using the laws of motion formulated by Newton, where the dynamics of a system are determined by the forces acting on it via \(\mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a}\). Although this approach is direct and physically intuitive, as systems become more complex, featuring constraints, multiple interacting bodies, or motion in curvilinear coordinates, the explicit analysis of forces can become cumbersome.
A powerful alternative formulation, developed in the eighteenth century by Lagrange, shifts the focus from forces to energies and from vector equations to scalar quantities. In Lagrangian mechanics, the motion of a system is derived from a single scalar function, the Lagrangian \(\mathcal{L}=T-V\), defined as the difference between kinetic and potential energy. Rather than using the forces to computing the future positions and velocities of the particle, the whole trajectory of the system is determined between two points for a given time. This trajectory correspond to a stationary point of the action \(S\), the time integral of the Lagrangian. This is the principle of least action, sometimes more accuraltly called principle of stationary action. This reformulation not only reproduces Newton's laws but also provides a more systematic and flexible framework, particularly well suited to constrained systems, generalized coordinates, and later developments in modern physics.


Here, two systems will be considered to illustrate the principle of stationary action: a particle in simple one dimensionnal motion under constant acceleration, and a particle in a gravity potential \(V(r)=-\frac{k}{r}\)

Euler-Lagrange equation

First, let's find the equation giving the trajectory that extremize the action. As stated before, the action is the time integral of the Lagrangian. It has dimension \([J]\cdot [s]\), and depends on the trajectory taken by the particle. It is then a functionnal, that takes a path \(\mathbf{x}\), and return a value. \begin{equation} S[\mathbf{x}] = \int_0^{T} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x},\dot{\mathbf{x}},t)\mathrm{d}t \end{equation} Similarly to function extremization, to find the stationary point of a functionnal, one consider a small displacement on the trajectory \(\mathbf{x}(t)=\mathbf{x}_0(t)+\delta\mathbf{x}(t)\)
The displacement \(\delta\mathbf{x}(t)\) is arbitrary, appart from the boundary conditions: The starting and end points of the trajectory are fixed, imposing \(\delta\mathbf{x}(0)=0, \delta\mathbf{x}(T)=0\). \begin{equation} \begin{split} S[\mathbf{x}_0+\delta\mathbf{x}]-S[\mathbf{x}_0] & = \int_0^{T} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}_0+\delta\mathbf{x},\dot{\mathbf{x}}_0+\dot{\delta\mathbf{x}},t) - \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}_0,\dot{\mathbf{x}}_0,t)\mathrm{d}t \\ & = \int_0^{T} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x}\delta\mathbf{x} + \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}}\dot{\delta\mathbf{x}} \mathrm{d}t \\ \end{split} \end{equation} By integration by part, and using the boundary conditions \(\delta\mathbf{x}(0)=0, \delta\mathbf{x}(T)=0\). \begin{equation} \begin{split} \int_0^{T} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}}\dot{\delta\mathbf{x}} \mathrm{d}t & = \underbrace{\left.\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}} \delta\mathbf{x}\right|_0^{T}}_{0} - \int_0^{T} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}}\delta\mathbf{x} \mathrm{d}t \end{split} \end{equation} \begin{equation} \begin{split} S[\mathbf{x}_0+\delta\mathbf{x}]-S[\mathbf{x}_0] & = \int_0^{T} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x}\delta\mathbf{x} - \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}}\delta\mathbf{x} \mathrm{d}t \\ & = \int_0^{T} \left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x} - \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}}\right) \delta\mathbf{x} \mathrm{d}t = 0 \\ \end{split} \end{equation} Imposing \(S[\mathbf{x}_0+\delta\mathbf{x}]-S[\mathbf{x}_0] = 0\) gives the condition for stationnary point.
Since the displacement is arbitrary, the only way to make the equation hold is to set \begin{equation} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}} - \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x} = 0 \end{equation} This is the Euler-Lagrange equation. Solving it gives the physical trajectory of the system.
In the simple case of cartesian coordinates and time independent potential, the Lagrangian is \(\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{\mathbf{x}}^2 - V(\mathbf{x})\).
The Euler-Lagrange equation gives \(m\ddot{\mathbf{x}} = -\mathbf{\nabla} V(\mathbf{x})\), which is exactly Newton's equation

Euler-Lagrange equation

Let's apply the principle of least action to a particle subject to contsnat gravitationnal field.
Let's consider a particle of mass \(m\) under constant downward acceleration \(mg\), and find its trajectory from \(z_1\) to \(z_2\) for a time \(T\).
The Euler-Lagrange equation gives naturally \(\ddot{z} = -g\)
Integrating two times gives \(z(t) = -\frac{1}{2}gt^2+v_0t+z_0\)
and imposing \(z(0)=z_1, z(T)=z_2\) gives the full trajectory \(z(t)=-\frac{1}{2}gt(t-T)+\frac{t}{T}(z_2-z_1)+z_1\)
But for the sake of illustration of the least action principle, let's compute the action of different trajectories. In particular, let's consider all possible parabola connecting the starting and end points in a time \(T\). Those trajectories have equation \(z_a(t) = \frac{1}{2}at(t-T)+\frac{t}{T}(z_2-z_1)+z_1\), with \(a\) a parameter.
\(\mathcal{L}=\frac{1}{2}m\dot{z_a}^2-mgz_a\) The action is \(S = \frac{mT^3}{24}a(a+2g) + \frac{m}{2}\frac{(z_2-z_1)^2}{T} - \frac{gmT}{2}(z_1+z_2)\)
Treating the action as a function of \(a\), we can simply find the extremum:
\begin{equation} \frac{\mathrm{d}S}{\mathrm{d}a} = \frac{1}{24}T^3\left(2a+2g\right) = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad a=-g \end{equation} As expected, the stationnary point of the action is such that \(a=-g\), retrieving the previous result for the trajectory \(z(t)\). This means that the physical trajectory is the one where the second derivative of position, or the acceleration, correspond to \(-g\), as stated by the Euler-Lagrange and Newton equation \(\ddot{z} = -g\).
