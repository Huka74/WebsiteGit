Brachistochrone

Check Euler-Lagrange for the derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations.

In this post, we will use the Euler-Lagrange equations to solve the famous Brachistochrone problem.
Originally considered by Galileo, the problem was later posed as a challenge in 1696 by the mathematician Johann Bernoulli. It quickly attracted the attention of many of the leading mathematicians of the time, including Newton, Leibniz, and Jakob Bernoulli. The problem asks a deceptively simple question:

"Given two points A and B in a vertical plane, what is the curve traced out by a point acted on only by gravity, which starts at A and reaches B in the shortest time."

At first glance, one might expect the fastest path to be a straight line, or a circular arc. Surprisingly, this is not the case. The solution is a specific curve known as a cycloid. In this post, we will solve the brachistochrone problem using the modern framework of the Euler-Lagrange equation.

Let's consider a particle subject to constant gravity \(g\), following a path \(y(x)\). The particle start from \(x_0,y_0\) at rest, and should reach a end point \(x_1,y_1\).
Using the conservation of energy, we can get the velocity at any point: \begin{equation} \begin{split} E_0 = mgy_0, \quad E =\frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgy(x)\\ mgy_0 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgy(x) \quad \Longrightarrow \quad v=\sqrt{2g(y_0-y(x))} \end{split} \end{equation} To obtain the time of travel, we consider a small element of the path \(ds\).
For a sufficiently small \(ds\), the velocity can be considered constant, and so the travel time along this element is \(dt = \frac{ds}{v}\).
To get the total travel time, we then integrate over the whole trajectory \(T = \int dt = \int\frac{ds}{v}\)
\begin{equation} T = \int \frac{ds}{v} = \int \frac{\sqrt{dx^2+dy^2}}{\sqrt{2g(y_0-y)}} = \left\{\begin{array}{l} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2g}} \int \frac{\sqrt{1+x'(y)^2}}{\sqrt{y_0-y}} dy\\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2g}} \int \frac{\sqrt{1+y'(x)^2}}{\sqrt{y_0-y(x)}} dx \end{array}\right. \end{equation} Here, to proceed we can either write \(ds=\sqrt{1+y'(x)^2}dx\) and consider \(y\) to be a function of \(x\), or \(ds=\sqrt{1+x'(y)^2}dy\) and consider \(x\) to be a function of \(y\). Although taking \(y(x)\) seems more natural, w will see later that it is easier to use \(x(y)\).
So we need to minimize \begin{equation} T = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2g}} \int \frac{\sqrt{1+x'(y)^2}}{\sqrt{y_0-y}} dy \end{equation} This is a similar problem to minimizing the action \begin{equation} S = \int \mathcal{L}(t,x,\dot{x}) dt \end{equation} with \(y\) taking the role of \(t\). The Euler-Lagrange equation gives \begin{equation} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}y}\frac{\partial}{\partial x'}\left(\frac{\sqrt{1+x'^2}}{\sqrt{y_0-y}}\right) - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\sqrt{1+x'^2}}{\sqrt{y_0-y}}\right) = 0 \end{equation} Thanks to the choice \(x(y)\), the second part gives 0, leaving only: \begin{equation} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}y}\frac{\partial}{\partial x'}\left(\frac{\sqrt{1+x'(y)^2}}{\sqrt{y_0-y}}\right) = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \frac{1}{\sqrt{y_0-y}}\frac{x'}{\sqrt{1+x'^2}}=C \end{equation} After isolating \(x'\), we have \begin{equation} dx=dy\sqrt{\frac{C^2(y_0-y)}{1-C^2(y_0-y)}} \end{equation} With the change of variables \(z=C^2(y_0-y)\) and then \(z=\sin^2\theta\) \begin{equation} dx = -\frac{1}{C^2}\sqrt{\frac{z}{1-z}}dz = -\frac{2}{C^2}\sin^2\theta d\theta \end{equation} We can integrating over \(\theta\) to get \(x(\theta)\) and invert the change of variable to get \(y(\theta)\) \begin{equation} \left\{\begin{array}{l} x(\theta) = x_0-\frac{1}{C^2}(\theta-\sin\theta\cos\theta)\\ y(\theta) = y_0-\frac{1}{C^2}\sin^2\theta \end{array}\right. \end{equation} Finally, defing \(\varphi=-2\theta\) and renaming the constant \(r=\frac{1}{2C^2}\), we get \begin{equation} \left\{\begin{array}{l} x(\varphi) = x_0+r(\varphi-\sin\varphi)\\ y(\varphi) = y_0-r(1-\cos\varphi) \end{array}\right. \end{equation} This parametric equation is the solution to the Brachistochrone problem, and correspond to a cycloid. It can be geometrically constructed by following a point on a rolling circle.
Because of the choice of integration constant, we have \((x(0),y(0))=(x_0,y_0)\). To fully solve the problem, we only need to fix the constant \(r\) and find a \(\varphi_1\) that coincide with reaching the end point \((x_1,y_1)\). However, this is not solvable analytically in general, and is solve numerically in the animation. Yet, in order to analytically compare the travel time with other paths, we will consider a specific end point that correspond to simple values for \(r\) and \(\varphi_1\).

Travel time comparison

The travel time of a particle on a brachistochrone can be analytically computed as a function of its parameter \(r\) and \(\varphi_1\), the angle at which the end point is reached. \begin{equation} \begin{split} \left\{\begin{array}{l} x(\varphi) = x_0+r(\varphi-\sin\varphi)\\ y(\varphi) = y_0-r(1-\cos\varphi) \end{array}\right. \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \left\{\begin{array}{l} dx = r(1-\cos\varphi)d\varphi\\ dy = r\sin\varphi d\varphi \end{array}\right. \\ \Longrightarrow \quad ds = \sqrt{dx^2+dy^2} = r\sqrt{2(1-\cos\varphi)}d\varphi = \sqrt{2r(y_0-y)}d\varphi \end{split} \end{equation} \begin{equation} T = \int \frac{ds}{v} = \int_0^{\varphi_1} \frac{\sqrt{2r(y_0-y)}}{\sqrt{2g(y_0-y)}}d\varphi = \sqrt{\frac{r}{g}}\varphi_1 \end{equation} Choosing \((x_0,y_0) = (0,0)\) and \((x_1,y_1) = (L\pi,-2L)\) gives \(r=L\) and \(\varphi_1=\pi\).
The travel time for this brachistochrone is \begin{equation} T = \pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} \end{equation} We can compute the travel time for other candidate paths and compare them. Two simple examples are:
\(\bullet\) A straight line connecting the two points.
\(\bullet\) A "L" shape path, where the particle first drops vertically to the height of the endpoint and then travels horizontally to reach it.
Strictly speaking, a particle moving under gravity cannot change direction abruptly at a sharp corner. To make this path physically meaningful, we imagine replacing the corner with a very small circular arc that smoothly redirects the particle from vertical motion to horizontal motion. In the limit where the radius of this arc goes to zero, the path approaches an idealized vertical drop followed by a horizontal segment.

On the other hand, the "L" shape path directly gain a lot a speed, but a the cost of a longer trajectory. The brachistochrone is a middle ground between gathering speed, while keeping the path short.

Tautochrone property

The brachistochrone, on top of begin the fastest trajectory to connect two points under gravity, also shows a nice property called "tautochrone". It means that for any starting point on the curve, all particles will reach the lowest point of the curve at the same time. This can be seen in the canvas by clicking on the third button. We will show this property here. First, the lowest point of the curve is reached at \(\varphi=\pi\). Using the previous formula, the travel time between an angle \(\varphi_0\) and \(\pi\) is \begin{equation} T = \int \frac{ds}{v} = \int_{\varphi_0}^{\pi} \frac{ds}{v} = \sqrt{\frac{r}{g}}\int_{\varphi_0}^{\pi} \sqrt{\frac{1-\cos\omega}{\cos\varphi_0-\cos\omega}}d\omega \end{equation} Previously, the starting point corresponded to \(\varphi=0\), simplifying the integral. Now the starting point is at \(\varphi=\varphi_0\), meaning that the energy is no longer \(\sqrt{2g(y_0-y)}=\sqrt{2gr(1-\cos\varphi)}\), but \(\sqrt{2g(y(\varphi_0)-y)}=\sqrt{2gr(\cos\varphi_0-\cos\varphi)}\)

Changing the variable \(z=\cos\omega\) \begin{equation} I(\varphi) = -\int_{c}^{\cos\varphi} \sqrt{\frac{1-z}{c-z}}\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-z^2}}dz = -\int_{c}^{\cos\varphi} \frac{1}{\sqrt{(c-z)(1+z)}}dz \end{equation} Completing the square gives \begin{equation} \begin{split} (c-z)(1+z) & = -z^2-(1-c)z+c\\ & = -\left(z+\frac{1-c}{2}\right)^2+\left(\frac{1-c}{2}\right)^2+c\\ & = -\left(z+\frac{1-c}{2}\right)^2+\left(\frac{1+c}{2}\right)^2\\ & = \left(\frac{1+c}{2}\right)^2\left(1-\left(\frac{2}{1+c}\right)^2\left(z+\frac{1-c}{2}\right)^2\right)\\ \end{split} \end{equation} Changing again the variable \(\lambda = \frac{2}{1+c}\left(z+\frac{1-c}{2}\right)\), we have \begin{equation} I(\varphi) = -\int_{c}^{\cos\varphi} \frac{1}{\sqrt{(c-z)(1+z)}}dz = \int_{1}^{\frac{2}{1+c}\left(\cos\varphi+\frac{1-c}{2}\right)} \frac{-1}{\sqrt{1-\lambda^2}}d\lambda \end{equation} \begin{equation} I(\varphi) = \arccos\left(\frac{2}{1+c}\left(\cos\varphi+\frac{1-c}{2}\right)\right) \end{equation}
\begin{equation} T(\varphi) = \sqrt{\frac{r}{g}}\arccos\left(\frac{2\cos\varphi+1-\cos\varphi_0}{1+\cos\varphi_0}\right) \end{equation} Meaning that \begin{equation} T(\pi) = \sqrt{\frac{r}{g}}\pi \quad \forall \varphi_0 \end{equation}
