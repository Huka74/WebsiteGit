Catenary/Hanging chain

Check Euler-Lagrange for the derivation of the Euler-Lagrange equations.

In this post, we will use the Euler-Lagrange equations to find the shape of a hanging chain.

Looking at power lines, or hanging chain, a natural question is "what shape does the chain takes ?".
One might be temped to say a parabola, but as we will see here, it is in fact a hyperbolic cosine.
Let's properly state the problem:
We have a chain of length \(L\) and uniform (linear) density \(\rho\), with both ends attached to \(x_0,y_0\) and \(x_1,y_1\). It is subject to a constant gravitationnal field \(g\). What is the shape of the chain ?

To find the physically correct shape, we need to find the one that minimize the potential energy. \begin{equation} E[y] = \int \rho gy ds \end{equation} We also have to impose that the length is \(L\) \begin{equation} \int ds = L \end{equation} We need to find \(y(x)\) that minimize the energy \(E[y]\) under the constraint \(\int ds = L\). Introducing Lagrange multiplier, we need to minimize \begin{equation} S = \int \rho g y ds - \lambda\left(\int ds - L\right) = \rho g\int \left(y-\frac{\lambda}{\rho g}\right) ds +\lambda L \end{equation} Just like in the Brachistochrone problem, we have to decide wether to write \(ds=\sqrt{1+y(x)'^2}dx\) and consider \(y\) to be a function of \(x\) or to write \(ds=\sqrt{1+x(y)'^2}dy\) and consider \(x\) to be a function of \(y\). The natural choice seems to be \(y(x)\), but once again, choosing \(x(y)\) will greatly simplify the following. \begin{equation} S = \rho g \int \left(y-\frac{\lambda}{\rho g}\right) \sqrt{1+x(y)'^2} dy \end{equation} Using the Euler-Lagrange equation with \(y\) taking the role of \(t\), and renaming the constant \(\frac{\lambda}{\rho g}=a\), gives \begin{equation} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}y}\frac{\partial}{\partial x'}\left((y-a)\sqrt{1+x'^2}\right) - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left((y-a)\sqrt{1+x'^2}\right) = 0 \end{equation} Thanks to the choice \(x(y)\), the second part gives 0, leaving only: \begin{equation} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}y}\frac{\partial}{\partial x'}\left((y-a)\sqrt{1+x'^2}\right) = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \frac{(y-a)x'}{\sqrt{1+x'^2}}=b \end{equation} After isolating \(x'\), we have \begin{equation} x'(y)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(\frac{y-a}{b}\right)^2-1}} \end{equation} We can now integrate over \(y\) to get \(x(y)\) and invert it to get \(y(x)\) \begin{equation} x(y)=b\text{ arccosh}\left(\frac{y-a}{b}\right)+c \quad \Longrightarrow \quad y(x)=b\cosh\left(\frac{x-c}{b}\right)+a \end{equation} with \(a\), \(b\) and \(c\) integration constants, fixed by imposing the boundary condition (fixing the ends of the chain) and imposing that the length is \(L\). \begin{equation} \left\{\begin{array}{l} y_0 = a+b\cosh\left(\frac{x_0-c}{b}\right)\\ y_1 = a+b\cosh\left(\frac{x_1-c}{b}\right)\\ L = \int_{x_0}^{x_1} \sqrt{1+y'^2}dx = \left.b\sinh\left(\frac{x-c}{b}\right)\right|_{x_0}^{x_1} \end{array}\right. \end{equation} Solving this gives \begin{equation} \left\{\begin{array}{l} 2b\sinh\left(\frac{x_1-x_0}{2b}\right) = \sqrt{L^2-(y_1-y_0)^2}\\ c = \frac{x_1+x_0}{2}-b\text{ arctanh}\left(\frac{y_1-y_0}{L}\right)\\ a = y_0-b\cosh\left(\frac{x_0-c}{b}\right) \end{array}\right. \end{equation} Once we find \(b\) with the first equation, \(c\) can be found with the second, and \(a\) with the third one. However, \(b\) can not be found analytically, and is solve numerically in the animation.

Notice that the sign of \(b\) is not fixed by the first equation, since replacing \(b\) with \(-b\) leaves it unchanged.
In fact, the system possesses the symmetry: \begin{equation} (a,b,c) \mapsto (y_0+y_1-a, -b, x_0+x_1-c) \end{equation} Therefore, if \((a,b,c)\) is a solution, then \((y_0+y_1-a, -b, x_0+x_1-c)\) is also a solution, one with the chain going downward and one upward. Geometrically, the second configuration is obtained from the first by the transformation \begin{equation} (x,y) \mapsto (x_0+x_1-x,y_0+y_1-y) \end{equation} which is the central symmetry about the mid point \(\left(\frac{x_0+x_1}{2},\frac{y_0+y_1}{2}\right)\).

It is easy to see that the true physical solution is the one with \(b\ge 0\), with the chain hanging below the two points. In fact \(b\ge 0\) correspond to the minimum of the action, whereas \(b\le 0\) correspond to the maximum.
To rigorously fix the sign of \(b\), one can check the stability of the solution of the Euler-Lagrange equation, i.e. if it is a minimum or a maximum. To do so, we can use the Legendre-Clebsch condition, stating that for a minimization problem \begin{equation} S = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} \mathcal{L}(t,x,\dot{x}) dt \end{equation} The solution \(x(t)\) is a minimum if \begin{equation} \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}}{\partial x'^2} \ge 0 \quad \forall t\in [t_0,t_1] \end{equation} In our case, \(\mathcal{L} = \rho g(y-a)\sqrt{1+x'^2}\) and \(y(x)=a+b\cosh\left(\frac{x-c}{b}\right)\), \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}}{\partial x'^2} & = \frac{\rho g(y-a)}{(1+x'^2)^{\frac{3}{2}}} \ge 0\\ & \Longrightarrow \rho g b\cosh\left(\frac{x-c}{b}\right) \ge 0\\ & \Longrightarrow \text{sign}(b) = \text{sign}(g)\\ \end{split} \end{equation} thus, fixing our last degree of freedom.
