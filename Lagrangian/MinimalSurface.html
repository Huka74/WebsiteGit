Minimal surface and soap film


Let us consider two identical circles facing each other, whose centers lie on the same perpendicular axis. What is the surface of smallest area that connects these two circles? At first glance, this question may seem somewhat artificial. However, problems involving minimal surfaces arise naturally in physics. A classic example is a thin soap film: such a film spontaneously adjusts its shape to minimize its surface area. This happens because the energy associated with surface tension is proportional to the area of the surface. As a result, the system seeks the configuration of lowest energy, that is, the one with minimal area. This explains why soap bubble are a perfect sphere, as it is the shape that minimize the surface area for a given volume.

In our case, the surface must connect the two given circles. A natural first guess might be a cylinder, formed by connecting corresponding points on the two circles with straight segments. However, this is not optimal as the surface area can be reduced if the connecting curves bend inward, moving closer to the central axis. By doing so, the intermediate rings of the surface become smaller. This introduces a trade-off: on one hand, we want to keep the paths between the circles short. On the other, we want to reduce the radius of the surface by pulling it inward toward the axis. The surface that balances this trade-off is called a catenoid, made by rotating a catenary curve around the axis. Let us solve this minimal surface with the Lagrangian formalism We denote \(z\) the axis going through both centers, and \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) the radii of the circles. The circles are separated by a distance \(L\). Because of the radial symmetry of the problem, we can only consider the function \(r(z)\) for the surface. The boundary condition impose \(r(0)=r_1\) and \(r(L)=r_2\). The surface area is then the sum of all the small circles along the \(z\) axis of thickness \(\mathrm{d}s\). \[S = \int 2\pi r \mathrm{d}s \] with \(\mathrm{d}s = \sqrt{\mathrm{d}z^2+\mathrm{d}r^2}\).
Minimizing \(S[r]\) is the same problem as the Catenary curve problem, where we had to minimize \(E[y] \propto \int y ds\), expect without constraint on the length. Check Catenary for the full derivation of the Catenary curve solution.
This means that our surface will be a revolution of the catenary curve around the axis. This is called a catenoid.
Using the Euler-Lagrange equation, we get for \(r(z)\) \[r(z)=A\cosh\left(\frac{z-B}{A}\right)\] with the boundary conditions to fix \(A\) and \(B\) \begin{equation} \left\{ \begin{array}{l} A\cosh\left(\frac{B}{A}\right) = r_1\\ A\cosh\left(\frac{L-B}{A}\right) = r_2\\ \end{array}\right. \end{equation} It is possible to solve for \(B\) as a function of \(A\), but the remain equation for \(A\) is transcendental.

Denoting \(x=\frac{L}{A}\) and \(y=\frac{B}{A}\), we can write the second equation as \[A\cosh(x-y) = A\cosh(x)\cosh(y)-A\sinh(x)\sinh(y)=r_2\] And inserting the first equation \(A\cosh(x)=r_1\) gives \[\cosh(x)-\sinh(x)\tanh(y)=\frac{r_2}{r_1}\] \[\tanh(y)=\frac{\cosh(x)-\frac{r_2}{r_1}}{\sinh(x)} = \tanh\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)+\frac{1-\frac{r_2}{r_1}}{\sinh(x)}\] \[B=A\:\text{arctanh}\left(\tanh\left(\frac{L}{2A}\right)+\frac{1-\frac{r_2}{r_1}}{\sinh\left(\frac{L}{A}\right)}\right)\] Using \(\cosh(\text{arctanh}(x)) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\), we a an equation for \(A\) that can be solve numerically \[A = r_1\sqrt{ 1 - \left(\tanh\left(\frac{L}{2A}\right)+\frac{1-\frac{r_2}{r_1}}{\sinh\left(\frac{L}{A}\right)}\right)^2}\]
Taking \(r_1=r_2=r\), we get \(B=\frac{L}{2}\), and the equation for \(A\) is \[A\cosh\left(\frac{L}{2A}\right) = r\] Denoting \(x=\frac{L}{2A}\) and \(\lambda=2\frac{r}{L}\) the equation becomes \[f_{\lambda}(x)=\frac{1}{x}\cosh(x)-\lambda=0\]

First, notice that sometimes the equation has two solutions. Those are two disctinct local minimal surfaces. In the animation, the smallest of those two solutions is shown.

Additionnaly, we can see that this equation has solution only for \(\lambda\) greater than a critical \(\lambda^*\). This critical \(\lambda^*\) can be computed by finding when the curve is tangent to the \(x\) axis. In other words, when the derivative, and the value of the function is 0: \[f_{\lambda^*}'(x) = -\frac{1}{x^2}\cosh(x)+\frac{1}{x}\sinh(x)=0\] \[\Longrightarrow x\tanh(x)=1\] This equation is independant of \(\lambda\) and can be solve numerically, giving \(x=c\approx 1.1997\).
Using the previous definition of \(x\), we get \(L^*=2cA\). Now imposing that \(f_{\lambda^*}(x)=0\) \[\lambda^*=\frac{1}{c}\cosh(c)\] \[L^*=r\frac{2c}{\cosh(c)}\approx 1.3255 r\]

The fact that no solution exists for \(L>L^*\) only means that our formalism could not find an extremal surfaces past this point. Physically, the minimal surface must still exits. In fact, after that point, the minimal surface consist of two discs, connected by only a line. This solution is on the extreme side of the afordmentionned trade-off, minimizing the radius of the surface. This is not a differentiable surface and cannot be represented by our formalism.
At the critical distance, the surface area of the catenoid is \(S=2\pi r^2 c\).

Let us compute the surface area of the catenoid. \[S=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_{0}^L r(z)dsd\theta = 2\pi \int_0^L r(z)\sqrt{1+r'(z)^2}dz\] with \(r(z)=A\cosh\left(\frac{z-\frac{L}{2}}{A}\right)\) \[\sqrt{1+r'(z)^2} = \cosh\left(\frac{z-\frac{L}{2}}{A}\right)\] \[S = 2\pi \int_0^L A\cosh^2\left(\frac{z-\frac{L}{2}}{A}\right)dz = \pi A\left(A\sinh\left(\frac{L}{A}\right)+L\right)\] At the critical point, \(L=r\frac{2c}{\cosh c}\), \(A=\frac{r}{\cosh c}\), giving \begin{equation} \begin{split} S & = \pi r^2 \frac{\sinh(2c)+2c}{\cosh^2 c}\\ & = 2\pi r^2\left(\tanh c + \frac{c}{\cosh^2 c}\right)\\ & = 2\pi r^2 \left(\tanh c+c(1-\tanh^2 c)\right)\\ & = 2\pi r^2 c\\ \end{split} \end{equation} recalling that \(c\) is defined by \(c\tanh c = 1\).
Compared to the two disks solution \(S=2\pi r^2\), we see that it has a smaller surface even before the catenoid stop existing. The catenoid's surface becomes greater than the two discs when \(L\gtrsim1.0553r\), but still exists as a local minimum.
