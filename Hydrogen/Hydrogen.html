Back to Home We will solve for the electronic orbitals of the Hydrogen atom with no quatum mechanic consideration except the Schrödinger equation. All quantization of the quantum numbers will naturally come out of the normalization condition.

The Schrödinger equation

The system consists of an electron and a proton interacting with the Coulomb force.
We denote the position, momentum and the mass of the electron \(\mathbf{r}_e,\mathbf{p}_e,m_e\) and of the proton \(\mathbf{r}_p,\mathbf{p}_p,m_p\).
The Hamiltonian is \[H(\mathbf{r}_e,\mathbf{r}_p,\mathbf{p}_e,\mathbf{p}_p) = \frac{\mathbf{p}_p^2}{2m_p}+\frac{\mathbf{p}_e^2}{2m_e}-\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{1}{|\mathbf{r}_p-\mathbf{r}_p|}\]
Considering the form of the potential in \(f(|\mathbf{r}_p-\mathbf{r}_p|)\), it would be advantageous to consider the new variable \(\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{r}_p-\mathbf{r}_e\), as well as \(\mathbf{R}=\frac{m_e\mathbf{r}_e+m_p\mathbf{r}_p}{m_e+m_p}\) and \(\mathbf{P}=M\dot{\mathbf{R}}=\mathbf{p}_p+\mathbf{p}_e\) the position and the momentum of the center of mass, with \(M=m_e+m_p\). \begin{split} \frac{\mathbf{P}^2}{2M} & = \frac{\mathbf{p}_p^2+\mathbf{p}_e^2+2\mathbf{p}_p\mathbf{p}_e}{2M} = \frac{\mathbf{p}_p^2}{2m_p}+\frac{\mathbf{p}_e^2}{2m_e}-\left(\frac{\mathbf{p}_p^2}{2m_p}-\frac{\mathbf{p}_p^2}{2M}+\frac{\mathbf{p}_e^2}{2m_e}-\frac{\mathbf{p}_e^2}{2M}-\frac{2\mathbf{p}_e\mathbf{p}_p}{2M}\right)\\ & = \frac{\mathbf{p}_p^2}{2m_p}+\frac{\mathbf{p}_e^2}{2m_e}-\left(\frac{m_p^2\dot{\mathbf{r}}_p^2}{2}\left(\frac{M-m_p}{m_pM}\right)+\frac{m_e^2\mathbf{r}_e^2}{2}\left(\frac{M-m_e}{m_pM}\right)-\frac{2m_pm_e\mathbf{r}_e\mathbf{r}_p}{2M}\right)\\ & = \frac{\mathbf{p}_p^2}{2m_p}+\frac{\mathbf{p}_e^2}{2m_e} - \frac{m_em_p}{m_p+m_e}\frac{(\dot{\mathbf{r}}_p-\dot{\mathbf{r}}_e)^2}{2}\\ \end{split} This form lead us to define \(\mathbf{p}=\mu(\dot{\mathbf{r}}_p-\dot{\mathbf{r}}_e)\) with \(\mu=\frac{m_pm_e}{m_p+m_e}\) and have \(\frac{\mathbf{p}_p^2}{2m_p}+\frac{\mathbf{p}_e^2}{2m_e} = \frac{\mathbf{P}^2}{2M} +\frac{\mathbf{p}^2}{2\mu}\).

With the new variables, the Hamiltonian becomes \begin{equation} H(\mathbf{r}_e,\mathbf{r}_p,\mathbf{p}_e,\mathbf{p}_p) = \frac{\mathbf{p}_p^2}{2m_p}+\frac{\mathbf{p}_e^2}{2m_e}-\frac{k}{|\mathbf{r}_p-\mathbf{r}_p|} \qquad H(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R},\mathbf{p},\mathbf{P}) = \frac{\mathbf{P}^2}{2M} +\frac{\mathbf{p}^2}{2\mu} - \frac{k}{|\mathbf{r}|} \end{equation}
We can, without loss of generality, put ourself in the referential of the center of mass, cancelling the global translation of the system (\(\mathbf{P}=0\)). Finally, the Hamiltonian to consider is \begin{equation} H(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{R},\mathbf{p},\mathbf{P}) = \frac{\mathbf{p}^2}{2\mu} - \frac{k}{|\mathbf{r}|} \end{equation}

The slider can be used to vary the ratio of mass of the two particles. Notice in the limit of large mass ratio, \(r_p\rightarrow R, \mu\rightarrow m_e\), (in our case, \(m_p/m_e\sim1000\))
Going to the quantum case, the Schrödinger equation associated to this Hamiltonian is \begin{equation} \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla^2-\frac{k}{r}\right)\psi=E\psi \end{equation}

Solving the equation

The potential only depends in \(|\mathbf{r}|\), it is therefore natural to use spherical coordinates. Writing the laplacian (\(\nabla^2\)) in spherical coordinate, we get: \begin{equation} \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\left(\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^2\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{1}{r^2\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\right)+\frac{1}{r^2\sin^2\theta}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\phi^2}\right)-\frac{k}{r}-E\right)\psi(r,\theta,\phi)=0 \end{equation}

Separation of variables

Using the separation of variables, we set the wavefunction to be equal to the product of three functions, each depending on only one coordinate: \(\psi(r,\theta,\phi)=R(r)\Theta(\theta)\Phi(\phi)\) \[\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^2\frac{\partial R}{\partial r}\right)\frac{1}{R}+\frac{1}{r^2\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial \Theta}{\partial\theta}\right)\frac{1}{\Theta}+\frac{1}{r^2\sin^2\theta}\frac{\partial^2\Phi}{\partial\phi^2}\frac{1}{\Phi}+\frac{2\mu}{\hbar^2}\left(\frac{k}{r}+E\right) = 0\] Multiplying by \(r^2\), and putting all depedancies on \(r\) on one side, we get: \[\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^2\frac{\partial R}{\partial r}\right)\frac{1}{R}+\frac{2\mu r^2}{\hbar^2}\left(\frac{k}{r}+E\right) = -\frac{1}{\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial \Theta}{\partial\theta}\right)\frac{1}{\Theta}-\frac{1}{\sin^2\theta}\frac{\partial^2\Phi}{\partial\phi^2}\frac{1}{\Phi} = \alpha\] The left-hand side only depend on \(r\), while the right-hand side does not. This means that both sides of equation must be a constant, here denoted \(\alpha\). This gives the following two equations: \begin{equation} \left\{\begin{array}{l} \frac{1}{R}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^2\frac{\partial R}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{2\mu r^2}{\hbar^2}\left(\frac{k}{r}+E\right) = \alpha \\ \frac{1}{\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial \Theta}{\partial\theta}\right)\frac{1}{\Theta}+\frac{1}{\sin^2\theta}\frac{\partial^2\Phi}{\partial\phi^2}\frac{1}{\Phi} = -\alpha \end{array}\right. \end{equation} We can use the same method for the second equation: multiply by \(\sin^2\theta\) and seperate terms depeding on \(\theta\) and \(\phi\). By doing so, we again find the left-hand side depending only on one variable while the right-hand depending only on the other. Setting both equal to a constant, we get: \[\frac{1}{\Theta}\sin\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial \Theta}{\partial\theta}\right) + \alpha\sin^2\theta = -\frac{1}{\Phi}\frac{\partial^2\Phi}{\partial\phi^2} = \beta\] Finally, we have a system of three equations, for the radial \(R(r)\), polar \(\Theta(\theta)\) and azimuthal \(\Phi(\phi)\) part of the wavefunction. \begin{equation}\label{eq:syst} \left\{\begin{array}{l} \frac{1}{R}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^2\frac{\partial R}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{2\mu r^2}{\hbar^2}\left(\frac{k}{r}+E\right) = \alpha \\ \frac{1}{\Theta}\sin\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial \Theta}{\partial\theta}\right) + \alpha\sin^2\theta -\beta = 0 \\ \frac{1}{\Phi}\frac{\partial^2\Phi}{\partial\phi^2} + \beta = 0 \end{array}\right.\\ \end{equation}

Solving the azimuthal part

Starting with the simplest equation, we solve for the azimuthal part \[\frac{\partial^2\Phi}{\partial\phi^2} = -\beta\Phi\qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad \Phi = Ae^{i\sqrt{\beta}\phi}+Be^{-i\sqrt{\beta}\phi}\] Because \(\phi\) represent the angle around the polar axis, we have to impose periodic boundary condition: \[\Phi(\phi) = \Phi(\phi+2\pi) \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad \beta=m^2,\:\: m\in\mathbb{Z}\] giving the first restriction on the constants, \(\beta=m^2\).

Solving the polar part

\[ \frac{1}{\Theta}\sin\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{\partial \Theta}{\partial\theta}\right) + \alpha\sin^2\theta - m^2 = 0\] \[ \Theta''+\frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta} \: \Theta' + \left(\alpha - \frac{m^2}{\sin^2\theta}\right)\Theta = 0\] The equation is singular at \(\theta=0,\pi\). By expanding around \(\theta\sim0\) (the analysis is the same around \(\theta\sim\pi\)) we get \[\Theta''+\frac{1}{\theta} \: \Theta' + \left(\alpha - \frac{m^2}{\theta^2}\right)\Theta = 0\] As we are looking for non divergent solution, we can write \(\Theta(\theta)\) as a power series. Let \(k\) be the power of the leading term of \(\Theta\), \(\Theta(\theta) = \theta^k+O(\theta^{k+1})\) (the leading term is the smallest power since we are looking around \(\theta\sim 0\))
Using this in the last equation, we get at first order \(k=\pm m\).
Once again, as we are looking for a wavefunction, we have to impose the solution to be \(L_2\) i.e. square integrable. This mean we can discard diverging solutions, and keep \(k=|m|\).
Knowing this behavior around the singular points, we can use the following Ansätz: \[\Theta(\theta)=C(\theta)\sin^{|m|}(\theta)\] Using this in the polar equation gives: \[\sin^2\theta C''(\theta)+\cos\theta\sin\theta(2|m|+1)C'(\theta)+C(\theta)(\alpha\sin^2\theta-|m|-|m|^2)\] Changing the variable \(x=\cos\theta,\:\: \Theta(\theta)=y(\cos\theta),\:\: \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}=-\sqrt{1-x^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial x} ,\:\: \frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2}=(1-x^2)\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}-x\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\)
the equation becomes \[(1-x^2)y''-2xy'+(\alpha-|m|(|m|+1))y=0\] This is the associated Legendre equation.
Using the Frobenius method, we set \(y(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_ix^i\), and putting this in the equation gives the following reccurence for the coefficients: \(a_{n}=a_{n-2}\frac{(n-2)(n-1+2|m|)+|m|(|m|+1)-\alpha}{n(n-1)}\) Notice that \(\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\frac{a_{n+2}}{a_n}=1\), so for the series to converge and the solution to not blow up at the poles \(x=\pm 1\), we impose that the series must terminate. This means that one of the term must vanish at some \(n\), and so \(n(n+1+2|m|)+|m|(|m|+1)-\alpha=0\). Rearanging the terms yields \(\alpha = (n+|m|)(n+|m|+1)\) for some \(n\in\mathbb{N}\). Therefore, for the solution to converge, we impose \(\alpha=l(l+1)\) with \(l\) an integer greater or equal to \(|m|\). The solution of the polar part is \(P_l^m(\cos\theta)\), the associated Legendre polynomials, with \(l\in\mathbb{N}, |m|\le l\)
Below, an iteractive way to see the termination condition of the series, and the convergence of the solution. The parameter \(\alpha\) can be changed, showing the effect on the coefficient of the series, and the solution of the equation.

Solving the radial part

\[\frac{1}{R}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^2\frac{\partial R}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{2\mu r^2}{\hbar^2}\left(\frac{k}{r}+E\right) = l(l+1)\] \[R''+\frac{2}{r}R'+\left(\frac{2\mu}{\hbar^2}\left(\frac{k}{r}+E\right) - \frac{l(l+1)}{r^2}\right)R = 0\] To solve this, we start by looking at asymptotic behaviors:
For \(r\longrightarrow 0\), keeping the dominant terms, the equation is
\[R''+\frac{2}{r}R' - \frac{l(l+1)}{r^2}R = 0\] Using the Ansätz \(R=r^\gamma\), we get \(\gamma(\gamma+1)=l(l+1) \Longrightarrow \gamma=l,-l-1\)
Keeping the non diverging solution, we have that \(R(r)\sim r^l\) for small \(r\).

For \(r\longrightarrow \infty\), keeping the dominant terms, the equation is
\[R''+\frac{2\mu E}{\hbar^2}R=0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad R''=\kappa^2 R\] with \(\kappa^2=-\frac{2\mu E}{\hbar^2} > 0\) since we are looking for bound states, i.e. \(E < 0\). \[R=Ae^{\kappa r}+Be^{-\kappa r}\] Again, the solution needs to be normalizable, leading us to discard the exponential increasing solution. \(R\sim e^{\kappa r}\) for large \(r\). Those asymptotic behaviors can be included in the final Ansätz for \(R(r)\): \[R(r)=r^l e^{-\kappa r}y(r)\] Using this in the original equation gives \[y''(r)+2\left(\frac{l+1}{r}-\kappa\right)y'(r)+\left(\frac{2\mu k}{\hbar^2 r}-2\frac{\kappa(l+1)}{r}\right)y(r)=0\] Finally, by defining a rescaled variable \(\rho=2\kappa r\), the equation becomes \[ y''(\rho)\rho+(2(l+1)-\rho))y'(\rho)+\left(\frac{\mu k}{\hbar^2\kappa}-l-1\right)y(\rho)=0\]
▶ Show detailed steps
Our Ansätz : \(R(r)=e^{-\kappa r}r^ly(r)\)
First derivative : \(R'(r)=e^{-\kappa r}r^l\left(y'(r)+y(r)\left(\frac{l}{r}-\kappa\right)\right)\)
Second derivative : \(R''(r)=e^{-\kappa r}r^l\left(y''(r)+2y'(r)\left(\frac{l}{r}-\kappa\right)+y(r)\left(\frac{l(l-1)}{r^2}-2\frac{\kappa l}{r}+\kappa^2\right)\right)\)

\[R''+\frac{2}{r}R'+\left(\frac{2\mu}{\hbar^2}\left(\frac{k}{r}+E\right) - \frac{l(l+1)}{r^2}\right)R = 0\] \[y''(r)+2\left(\frac{l+1}{r}-\kappa \right)y'(r)+\left(\frac{2\mu k}{\hbar^2 r}-2\frac{\kappa(l+1)}{r}\right)y(r)=0\] With the change of variable \(\rho=2\kappa r,\:\: \partial_r=2\kappa\partial_\rho,\:\: \partial_r^2=4\kappa^2\partial_\rho^2\) \[ y''(\rho)\rho+(2(l+1)-\rho))y'(\rho)+\left(\frac{\mu k}{\hbar^2\kappa}-l-1\right)y(\rho)=0\]
This last form is the generalized Laguerre equation \[xy''+(\alpha+1-x)y'-\lambda y=0\] with \(\alpha=2l+1\) and \(\lambda=\frac{\mu k}{\hbar^2\kappa}-l-1\)
Using the Frobenius method \(y(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_nx^n\), we get \(a_n=a_{n-1}\frac{n-1-\lambda}{n(n+\alpha)}\) Having the coefficients scaling like \(a_n\sim 1/n\), the series will grows exponentially in \(x\) if it does not terminate. So, as before, to ensure that the solution will be normalizable, we impose the series to termite, i.e. \(\lambda\) must be a positive integer. However, to be rigorous, one should compare the growth rate of the series to \(e^{-x/2}\), as it appears in the rest of the radial solution.
After some growth rate estimation, one can find that \(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_nx^n \sim x^{-\lambda-\alpha-3/2}e^{x}\) which would still grows exponentially after adding the \(e^{-x/2}\) factor.
▶ Show detailed steps
\[a_n=a_{n-1}\frac{n-1-\lambda}{n(n+\alpha)} = \frac{a_{0}}{n!}\frac{\Gamma(n-\lambda)}{\Gamma(-\lambda)}\frac{\Gamma(\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}\] Using the Stirling approximation : \(\Gamma(x)\overset{x\rightarrow\infty}{\longrightarrow}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{x}}x^xe^{-x}\) and recalling that \(\lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\left(1+\frac{a}{x}\right)^x=e^a\) \[ \frac{\Gamma(x+a)}{\Gamma(x+b)}\overset{x\rightarrow\infty}{\longrightarrow}\sqrt{\frac{x+b}{x+a}}\frac{(x+a)^{x+a}}{(x+b)^{x+b}}e^{b-a} = \sqrt{\frac{x+a}{x+b}}\left(\frac{1+\frac{a}{x}}{1+\frac{b}{x}}\right)^x\frac{(x+a)^{a}}{(x+b)^{b}}e^{b-a} \overset{x\rightarrow\infty}{\longrightarrow} x^{a-b}\] With this approximation, we have that for large \(n\) \[a_n=a_{0}\frac{\Gamma(\alpha+1)}{\Gamma(-\lambda)}\frac{1}{n!}\frac{\Gamma(n-\lambda)}{\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)}\sim \frac{n^{-\lambda-\alpha-1}}{n!}\] Taking a lower bound on the series, and using again the Stirling approximation for the factorial, we get the following growth rate \[\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_nx^n\ge a_kx^k\overset{k\sim [x]}{\sim} x^{-\lambda-\alpha-1}\frac{x^x}{x!}\sim x^{-\lambda-\alpha-3/2}e^{x}\]
This impose \(\lambda=\frac{\mu k}{\hbar^2\kappa}-l-1\) to be an integer in order to have a normalizable solution.
Setting \(\frac{\mu k}{\hbar^2\kappa}=n\in\mathbb{N}\), we have that \(n>l\)
Finally, we have our three quantum numbers with their condition: \begin{equation} \begin{array}{l} n,& 1\le n \\ l,& 0\le l < n \\ m,& -l\le m \le l\\ \end{array} \end{equation} Replacing the expression for \(\kappa\), we can obtain the energy levels of the hydrogen: \begin{equation} \frac{\mu k}{\hbar^2\kappa}=n \quad \Longrightarrow \quad E=-\frac{\mu e^4}{32\pi^2\epsilon_0^2\hbar^2}\frac{1}{n^2}=-\frac{R_y}{n^2} \end{equation} computing the value for \(R_y\) gives \(-13.59 \text{ eV}\) (\(-13.6 \text{ eV}\) if we use the mass of the electron istead of the reduced mass)

Putting everything together

In the end, the solution to the electronic orbitals of the Hydrogen atom is \begin{equation} \psi(r,\theta,\phi) = C \rho^l e^{-\rho/2} L^{2l+1}_{n-l-1}(\rho) P_l^m(\cos(\theta))e^{im\phi} \end{equation} with \(\rho=\frac{\mu e^2}{2\pi\epsilon_0\hbar^2 n}r=\frac{2}{a_0^* n}r\), \(\:a_0^*\) the reduced Bohr radius, \(\:\:L^{2l+1}_{n-l-1}\) being the generalized Laguerre polynomials, \(\:\:P_l^m\) the associated Legendre polynomials, and \(C\) the normalization constant.
