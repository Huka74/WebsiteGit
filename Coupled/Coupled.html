Coupled systems

This is the content for Part 1.

Back to Home Let \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) be two masses attached to two opposite walls and each other with springs. The stiffness of the springs are \(k_0,k_1,k_2\), and their equilibirum lengths \(L_0,L_1,L_2\). We set the distance separating the two walls to be \(L=L_0+L_1+L_2\).
Below is an animation showing the dynamic of the system. You can drag and release the masses to change the initial condition.
Finding the evolution of the system might seem complicated, where the dynamic of the first mass influence the second, and vice-versa, but with a bit of algebra, we can broke down the system into simple harmonic motions.

The equations of motion are \[ \begin{array}{l} m_1\ddot{x}_1 = -k_0(x_1-L_0) \hspace{0.95cm} +k_1(x_2-x_1-L_1)\\ m_2\ddot{x}_2 = -k_2(x_2-L_0-L_1) -k_1(x_2-x_1-L_1)\\ \end{array} \] As often when solving for the motion, it is usefull to change the variables to take the origin at the equilibirum position : \(x_1 \leftarrow x_1-L_0, \:\: x_2 \leftarrow x_2-L_0-L_1\). The equations of motion becomes \[ \begin{array}{l} m_1\ddot{x}_1 = -k_0x_1 +k_1(x_2-x_1)\\ m_2\ddot{x}_2 = -k_2x_2-k_1(x_2-x_1)\\ \end{array} \] Given the form of the equations, it is natural to rewrite the system of differential equations in a matrix form. \[ \left(\begin{array}{cc} \ddot{x}_1\\ \ddot{x}_2 \end{array}\right) = \left(\begin{array}{cc} -\frac{k_0+k_1}{m_1} & \frac{k_1}{m_1}\\ \frac{k_1}{m_2} & -\frac{k_1+k_2}{m_2} \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{cc} x_1\\ x_2 \end{array}\right) \] \[ \ddot{\mathbf{X}} = A\mathbf{X} \] Diagonalizing the matrix A with the transition matrix \(P\) would give us new coordinates \(\mathbf{Q}=P^{-1}\mathbf{X}\), that evolve according to \[ \begin{array}{ccc} P^{-1}\ddot{\mathbf{X}} & = & P^{-1}AP & P^{-1}\mathbf{X}\\ \ddot{\mathbf{Q}} & = & D & \mathbf{Q} \end{array} \] \[ \left(\begin{array}{cc} \ddot{Q}_1\\ \ddot{Q}_2 \end{array}\right) = \left(\begin{array}{cc} \lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2 \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{cc} Q_1\\ Q_2 \end{array}\right) \] In other words, with these new variables the equation of motions only involves one variable at a time, meaning that the dynamic is now decoupled !
Moreover, it is easy to solve for \(Q_1\) and \(Q_2\) given the sign of the eigenvalues.
Indeed, the three cases are :
\(\bullet\quad \lambda > 0\), \(Q(t)=Ae^{\sqrt{\lambda}x}+Be^{-\sqrt{\lambda}x}\)
\(\bullet\quad \lambda=0\), \(Q(t)=A+Bt\)
\(\bullet\quad \lambda < 0\), \(Q(t)=A\cos(\sqrt{-\lambda}x)+B\sin(\sqrt{-\lambda}x)\)
Notice that only \(\lambda<0\) gives bounded solutions.

In our case, \(\text{det}(A) > 0\) and \(\text{Tr}(A)<0\), so both eigenvalues are negative.

Recall that the trace and the determinant is respectively the sum and the product of the eigenvalues. \[ \text{Tr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n\lambda_i \qquad \qquad \text{det}(A) = \prod_{i=1}^n\lambda_i \] For a \(2\times2\) matrix, knowing the sign of those two quantities gives us enough information to get the signs of the eigenvalues. In our case, having a positive determinant means that both eigenvalues have the same sign, and the sign is given by the trace.

Here is a table summarizing the different cases. (We can always rename the eigenvalues such that \(|\lambda_1|\le|\lambda_2|\)) \[ \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|} \hline & \text{Tr}>0 & \text{Tr}=0 & \text{Tr}<0\\ \hline \text{det}>0 & \lambda_1>0,\lambda_2>0 & \text{impossible} & \lambda_1<0,\lambda_2<0\\ \hline \text{det}=0 & \lambda_1=0,\lambda_2>0 & \lambda_1=0,\lambda_2=0 & \lambda_1=0,\lambda_2<0\\ \hline \text{det} <0 & \lambda_1<0,\lambda_2>0 & \lambda_1=-\lambda_2<0 & \lambda_1>0,\lambda_2<0\\ \hline \end{array} \] Important: this is valid only for real eigenvalues.
For example, \(\text{det}>0\) and \(\text{Tr}=0\) is possible with \(\lambda_1=i\alpha\) and \(\lambda_2=-i\alpha\)
Writing \(\lambda_i = -\omega_i^2\) to explicitly show the sign, one can see in the equations of motion that the eigenvalues are related to the frequencies of harmonic motions. \[ \begin{array}{cc} Q_1(t)=Q_{1_0}\cos(\omega_1t)+\frac{V_{1_0}}{\omega_1}\sin(\omega_1t) \\ Q_2(t)=Q_{2_0}\cos(\omega_2t)+\frac{V_{2_0}}{\omega_2}\sin(\omega_2t) \end{array} \] After diagonalization, we can get the frequencies \(\omega_1=\sqrt{\beta-\gamma}\), \(\:\omega_2=\sqrt{\beta+\gamma}\)
denoting \(\alpha=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{k_0+k_1}{m_1}-\frac{k_1+k_2}{m_2}\right)\), \(\beta=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{k_0+k_1}{m_1}+\frac{k_1+k_2}{m_2}\right)\) and \(\gamma=\sqrt{\alpha^2+\frac{k_1^2}{m_1m_2}}\).
It also gives us the transition matrix \(P\), allowing us to go from the original to the normal coordinates.
\[ P = \left(\begin{array}{c} (\alpha-\gamma) & (\alpha+\gamma)\\ -\frac{k_1}{m_2} & -\frac{k_1}{m_2}\\ \end{array}\right) \qquad P^{-1} = \frac{1}{2\gamma}\left(\begin{array}{c} -1 & -\frac{m_2}{k_1}(\alpha+\gamma)\\ 1 & \frac{m_2}{k_1}(\alpha-\gamma)\\ \end{array}\right) \] Using \(\mathbf{Q}_0 = P^{-1}\mathbf{X}_0\), we can find the initial condition for the normal coordinates and find the their full evolution \[ \begin{align} Q_1(t)=\left((\alpha-\gamma)x_{1_0} - \frac{k_1}{m_2}x_{2_0}\right)\cos(\omega_1t)+\frac{1}{\omega_1}\left((\alpha-\gamma)v_{1_0} - \frac{k_1}{m_2}v_{2_0}\right)\sin(\omega_1t) \\ Q_2(t)=\left((\alpha+\gamma)x_{1_0} - \frac{k_1}{m_2}x_{2_0}\right)\cos(\omega_2t)+\frac{1}{\omega_2}\left((\alpha+\gamma)v_{1_0} - \frac{k_1}{m_2}v_{2_0}\right)\sin(\omega_2t) \end{align} \] Finally, using \(\mathbf{X} = P\mathbf{Q}\), we get the solution of the problem. \[ \begin{array}{l} x_1(t) = \frac{1}{2\gamma}\left(Q_2(t)-Q_1(t)\right)\\ x_2(t) = \frac{1}{2\gamma}\frac{m_2}{k_1}\left((\alpha-\gamma)Q_2(t)-(\alpha+\gamma)Q_1(t)\right) \end{array} \]
