Each pixel of this canvas represent a double pendulum, with different initial angles. \(\theta_1\), in the \(x\) axis, and \(\theta_2\), in the \(y\) axis, go form \(-\pi\) to \(\pi\). Both initial angular velocities \(\dot{\theta}_1\) and \(\dot{\theta}_2\) are set to be \(0\).
The "zoom" button can be used to click and drag to zoom on the canvas, the "reset" button reset the simulation time to \(0\) and the drop down menu can be used to change what is shown for each pendulum

1. Shows the value of \(\theta_2\), from cyan for large negative values, to dark blue near 0 and then to pink for large positive value. This can be used to find stability islands, where the \(\theta_2\) stays near 0. However, this does not mean that blue points necessarly are periodic, nor that non blue points are not periodic. A better metric is the second option.

2. Shows an estimation of the Lyapunov exponent. It shows how quickly two pendulums starting with similar initial conditions diverge from one another. Green shows small Lyapunov exponent, stable initial condition, and blue shows large Lyapunov exponent, chaotic initial conditions.

3. Shows in which quadrant the second mass is: green when the second mass is on the top right of the first one, blue on the bottom right, pink on the bottom left, and yellow on the top left.

4. Shows the energy. Notice, as expected by energy conservation, that the color do not change.

We can clearly see a large stable zone on the center, corresponding to low energy pendulums.
However, when searching around using the \(\theta_2\) and Lyapunov estimation, one can find some isolated stability islands, with interesting trajectories.
